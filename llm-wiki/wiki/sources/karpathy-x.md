# Karpathy x: AI Capability Perception Gap

> Source file: `raw/Karpathy x.md`

## One-Paragraph Summary

This source argues that public arguments about AI capability are often confused because different groups are experiencing very different model tiers, product surfaces, and domains of use. Casual users may judge AI from outdated or free consumer experiences, while technical professionals using frontier agentic systems such as [[openai-codex]] and [[claude-code]] in programming, math, or research encounter much sharper capability gains. The result is a widening [[ai-capability-perception-gap]] shaped by [[peaky-capabilities]], [[verifiable-rewards]], and commercial incentives.

## Key Claims

1. Many people underestimate current AI capability because their impressions were formed from older or free-tier systems rather than the latest frontier products.
2. The most dramatic recent gains are concentrated in technical domains such as programming, math, and research, not in generic search, advice, or writing tasks.
3. Reinforcement learning improves faster in domains with explicit, verifiable reward signals, such as passing tests or solving formal tasks.
4. Companies prioritize technical agentic domains partly because they produce more business value than softer consumer use cases.
5. AI discourse is fragmented because casual users and technical frontier users are both observing real phenomena, but from different parts of the capability distribution.

## User Groups In The Source

| Group | Typical exposure | Resulting impression |
|------|------------------|----------------------|
| Casual or outdated users | Free tier, older models, viral failure clips | AI still looks flaky and overhyped |
| Technical frontier users | Paid state-of-the-art agentic models in coding, math, research | AI progress feels steep, practical, and high-stakes |

The source claims these groups are often "speaking past each other," which is the core of [[ai-capability-perception-gap]].

## Product Framing

- [[advanced-voice-mode]] is used as an example of a public-facing experience that can visibly fail on simple tasks.
- [[openai-codex]] and [[claude-code]] are used as examples of frontier agentic systems that perform much better in technical domains.

This produces the comparison captured in [[consumer-ai-vs-frontier-agentic-models]].

## Why The Source Thinks Capability Is Uneven

The source offers two explanations for uneven progress:

1. Some domains expose clean, machine-checkable rewards, making them easier to train with reinforcement learning
2. Some domains are commercially more valuable, so product teams allocate more effort to them

Those arguments are filed as [[verifiable-rewards]] and [[peaky-capabilities]].

## Notable Phrases Worth Preserving

- "growing gap in understanding of AI capability"
- "peaky" capability gains in technical domains
- "AI Psychosis" as the author's label for the intensity of belief among technical frontier users

The last phrase is source-specific and should not be generalized beyond this source without additional evidence. `(single-source)`
